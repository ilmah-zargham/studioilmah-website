Project context and constraints

DISCOVER

The problem

The VSR professorship pages at TU Chemnitz were built on an outdated university-wide template that limited layout flexibility, visual hierarchy, and interaction patterns. Over time, the pages became text-heavy, visually inconsistent, and difficult for students and external visitors to scan efficiently.

Key information such as contact details, course guidance, exam procedures, and research resources was buried in long paragraphs, low-quality images, and unstructured lists. Important steps were often explained only through dense text or large screenshots, making the content feel overwhelming rather than supportive.

Because the professorship was required to follow TU Chemnitz’s global CMS structure, neither the overall layout nor the wording of the content could be changed. The challenge was therefore to significantly improve clarity, readability, and usability within strict technical and institutional constraints.

The users

The primary users were students who regularly visited education, exams, guidelines, and FAQ pages to find clear instructions quickly. Secondary users included prospective students, research partners, and university staff who needed reliable contact and location information.

Their main need was simple: fast, scannable access to the right information without reading long paragraphs. Many pages were accessed under time pressure, especially before exams or during course planning.

Constraints & limitations

The redesign took place within the TU Chemnitz university CMS, which imposed strict layout, branding, and structural limitations.

  • Fixed university-wide layout and navigation
  • Limited editable content area
  • Text content could not be rewritten or shortened
  • Academic terminology and structure had to remain unchanged
  • Visual alignment with TU Chemnitz branding was required

As a result, the focus was on improving usability through visual hierarchy, spacing, grouping, and interaction patterns rather than changing content or layout foundations.

Problems in the legacy pages

The problem space

Although the VSR professorship pages contained valuable academic information, the way this content was presented created significant usability challenges. Over time, the pages had evolved without a consistent visual or structural system, forcing users to read large amounts of text to locate even basic information.

Poor scannability

Most pages relied on long, uninterrupted paragraphs with minimal visual separation. Headings were not clearly differentiated from body text, and related information was rarely grouped together.

Inconsistent visual language

Icons varied in style, colors were used without a clear system, and images differed widely in quality and resolution. This inconsistency reduced clarity and weakened the professional appearance of the professorship pages.

Information overload

Important actions and instructions were often embedded inside dense paragraphs. Step-based processes were presented as continuous text, and large screenshots were displayed inline by default, creating visual clutter and increasing cognitive load.

Low perceived quality

Outdated banners, low-resolution diagrams, and old campus maps negatively affected how the pages were perceived. Even though the content itself was reliable, the visual presentation reduced trust and engagement.

Why this mattered

These issues were particularly impactful for students, who often accessed the pages under time pressure. Poor scannability and visual overload directly affected their ability to prepare, plan, and make informed decisions efficiently.

Define

DEFINE

Goals and success criteria

Based on the identified usability issues and institutional constraints, the redesign focused on improving how information is structured, accessed, and perceived rather than changing the content itself.

Primary goals

  • Improve readability and scannability of content-heavy academic pages
  • Help students locate relevant information without reading entire pages
  • Create a consistent visual and structural system across all VSR pages
  • Reduce cognitive load for task-oriented pages (exams, guidelines, course info)
  • Maintain full compatibility with TU Chemnitz CMS and branding rules

Success criteria

  • Key sections and actions are recognizable within seconds of scanning
  • Related information is grouped and visually separated
  • Patterns (headings, icons, layouts) stay consistent across pages
  • Important instructions are easy to find without excessive scrolling
  • The redesign feels modern while respecting institutional constraints

Design approach and decisions

Given the fixed university-wide template, the redesign followed a structure-first UX approach. Instead of attempting a full visual overhaul, the focus was placed on how users scan, navigate, and complete tasks on information-heavy pages.

  • Strengthened hierarchy using spacing, headings, and grouping
  • Applied repeatable patterns to create predictability across pages
  • Added visual cues to support dense text without rewriting content
  • Designed within the existing template rather than fighting it
  • Treated constraints as boundaries that guide consistent decisions

Information structure improvements

A major part of the redesign focused on reorganizing information visually so students and visitors could understand each page quickly and move directly to what they need.

  • Separated content into scannable sections with clear anchors
  • Grouped related information into recognizable blocks and cards
  • Reduced perceived density through spacing and consistent layouts
  • Improved wayfinding by highlighting key actions and decision points
  • Standardized section patterns across all VSR pages for consistency

Develop

DEVELOP

Visual system and UI patterns

To modernize the VSR pages within the fixed TU Chemnitz CMS layout, I created a lightweight visual system that improves consistency, hierarchy, and perceived quality without changing the underlying structure.

Color and identity

I aligned the redesign with the university’s global header by using TU Chemnitz green for structure and navigation-related elements, while using the VSR signature orange for emphasis and key highlights. This kept the pages visually integrated with the university platform while giving the professorship a recognizable identity.

Typography and hierarchy

Since text could not be rewritten, readability improvements came from hierarchy: clearer headings, more spacing between sections, and stronger grouping. The goal was to help users scan first, then read only what is relevant.

Reusable UI patterns

I introduced consistent patterns across pages, such as section blocks, card-like groupings, and clear call-to-action links. Using repeatable layouts reduced cognitive load because users could predict where to find information on different pages.

  • Consistent icon style to create visual coherence across pages
  • Spacing and grouping to separate topics and reduce perceived density
  • Card-style layouts for contact and travel information
  • Clear CTA links for profiles, downloads, and next steps
  • Progressive disclosure pattern for long step-based instructions
DEVELOP

Key page redesigns

Instead of redesigning isolated screens, the focus was on applying the visual system consistently across the most frequently used VSR pages. Priority was given to pages where students experienced the highest friction and time pressure.

Contact page

The legacy contact page presented information as a long list, making it difficult to distinguish roles and find the right person quickly.

  • Introduced clear grouping for roles and responsibilities
  • Applied card-style layouts for better separation
  • Added direct profile links for faster access to detailed information
  • Improved hierarchy using spacing and consistent emphasis

Visiting us page

The original “Visiting us” page relied on a low-resolution map and unstructured travel information, which made orientation difficult for external visitors.

  • Redesigned the campus map manually for clarity and resolution
  • Used VSR orange to highlight relevant buildings and routes
  • Structured travel information by transport type
  • Improved visual clarity and spatial understanding

Education page

Previously, the education page consisted almost entirely of text with outdated icons and no visual entry points.

  • Added a custom banner to introduce the page visually
  • Designed a custom illustration to support the academic theme
  • Improved hierarchy and spacing between sections
  • Highlighted key actions relevant to students
DEVELOP

Interactions (FAQ image expand)

On FAQ and exam-related pages, step-by-step instructions were previously displayed as dense text accompanied by large inline screenshots. This created visual clutter and made it difficult to focus on individual steps.

To address this, I introduced a progressive disclosure interaction that reveals screenshots only when users actively request them.

  • Converted steps into clearly numbered sequences
  • Moved screenshots behind click-based interactions
  • Reduced visual noise on initial page load
  • Allowed users to focus on one step at a time
DEVELOP

Implementation notes

The redesign was implemented directly within the existing TU Chemnitz CMS, which required close alignment between design decisions and technical feasibility.

  • Worked within a fixed HTML structure and predefined content areas
  • Used custom CSS to improve spacing, grouping, and visual hierarchy
  • Ensured consistency across all VSR pages using shared patterns
  • Balanced visual improvements with performance and maintainability

This approach ensured that the redesigned pages could be maintained by the professorship without introducing fragile or complex dependencies.

DELIVER

Outcomes and improvements

The redesign significantly improved the clarity and usability of the VSR professorship pages while fully respecting the technical and institutional constraints of the TU Chemnitz platform.

  • Improved scannability across all pages through clearer hierarchy and spacing
  • Reduced visual clutter by restructuring dense content and hiding non-essential elements
  • Created a consistent visual language across all VSR pages
  • Improved wayfinding for students, especially on exams and guidelines pages
  • Modernized the perceived quality of the professorship website

Although no formal usability testing was conducted, feedback from students and staff indicated that information was easier to find and pages felt clearer and more structured than before.

The redesign has been fully implemented within the existing CMS and is actively used by students and staff as part of the TU Chemnitz website.
